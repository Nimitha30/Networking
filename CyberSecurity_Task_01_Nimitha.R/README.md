## Part B — Password Security Review

Covers five key areas of password security:

1. **What Makes a Strong Password** — Length (12–16+ characters), complexity (uppercase, lowercase, numbers, symbols), unpredictability, and randomness.
2. **Why Passwords Should Be Unique** — Prevents credential stuffing attacks and contains the blast radius of any single breach.
3. **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)** — Requires two or more verification factors: something you know, something you have, and something you are.
4. **Risks of Password Reuse** — Enables credential stuffing, chain breaches, and delayed detection of compromised accounts.
5. **Password Policy (8 Best Practices)** — Minimum 12 characters, mixed character types, no personal information, MFA enabled, unique passwords per account, and use of a password manager.

## Part D — Cyber Threat Research

Five major cyber threats were researched, each with a definition, a real-world example, and prevention methods.

### 1. Phishing
Attackers impersonate trusted sources to steal credentials or personal information via fake emails, messages, or websites.
**Example:** 2016 Google Docs phishing attack that affected millions of users.

### 2. Malware
Malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to systems — includes viruses, trojans, spyware, and worms.
**Example:** 2017 WannaCry attack that infected 200,000+ computers across 150 countries.

### 3. Ransomware
A type of malware that encrypts victim files and demands a ransom payment (typically cryptocurrency) for the decryption key.
**Example:** 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack by DarkSide, resulting in a $4.4 million ransom payment.

### 4. Social Engineering
Psychological manipulation that tricks individuals into revealing confidential information or taking security-compromising actions.
**Example:** 2020 Twitter hack where employees were impersonated to gain internal access and hijack high-profile accounts.

### 5. Data Breach
Unauthorized access to confidential data stored by an organization, which may include passwords, financial records, or personal details.
**Example:** 2013 Yahoo breach exposing data from all 3 billion user accounts.

## Part F — Security Reflection

### Risks Identified in Personal Digital Habits
- Password reuse across multiple accounts (Google, Instagram, etc.)
- No MFA enabled on any account
- No recovery emails configured on accounts

### Immediate Security Improvements Planned
- Enable MFA on all accounts without delay
- Set unique, complex passwords (12+ characters) for every account
- Use a trusted password manager (e.g. Bitwarden or Google Password Manager)
- Configure recovery emails on all accounts
- Develop the habit of verifying email senders before clicking any links

### Why Cybersecurity Matters
Cybersecurity is critical because nearly every aspect of modern life — banking, healthcare, education, communication — exists online. Threats are growing more sophisticated, yet most attacks can be prevented through consistent security habits. Awareness is the first and most powerful line of defence.

---

## Key Takeaways

- A single reused password can expose all your accounts at once.
- MFA is one of the most impactful single steps anyone can take to secure accounts.
- Human behaviour is often the weakest link — social engineering exploits trust, not software.
- Regular security audits (like this one) are essential for identifying and fixing personal vulnerabilities.
